Theorem: Let \(\{A_i\}_{i \in I}\) and \(\{B_i\}_{i \in I}\) be two families of mutually disjoint set over the same index set I. If \(A_i \sim B_i\) for all \(i\in I\) then \(\cup_{i \in I} A_i \sim \cup_{i\in I} B_i\).

Proof: Assume that \(A_i \sim B_i\), then this means there exists a bijection \(f_i: A_i \sim B_i\) for all \(i \in I\). Consider the rule, \(f: \cup_{i \in I}A_i \sim \cup_{i\in I} B_i\) defined as \(f(x) = f_i(x)\).

Claim: \(f\) is a well-defined function

We now claim that \( f \) is a well defined function. Since \( \cup_{i\in I}\{A_i\} \) is a set of mutually disjoint sets and using the definition of \(f\), we see that for every \(x \in \cup_{i\in I}\{A_i\} \), \(x \) has an image in \(\cup_{i \in I}\{B_i\} \). This means \(f\) is a well defined function.

Claim: \(f\) is injective

Next, we now show that \(f\) is injective. That is if \(x_1, x_2 \in \cup_{i \in I}\{A_i\} \) such that \(f(x_1) = f(x_2) \) then \(x_1 = x_2\). By direct proof, let's assume that \(x_1, x_2 \in \cup_{i \in I}\{A_i\} \) such that \(f(x_1) = f(x_2)\), then by definition of \(f\), f_i(x_1) = f_j (x_2) [Note: Despite the equality, we cannot assume that f will give us the same function \(f_i\), \(f_j\) just yet.] Since \(f(x_1) = f(x_2)\) and \(\{A_i\}\) are mutually disjoint sets, then \(x_1\) and \(x_2\) must be in the same \(A_i\). So \(i = j\). Since \(f_i\) is injective and \(f_i(x_1) = f_j(x_2)\), this means \(x_1 = x_2\). Therefore, \(f\) must be injective.

Claim: \(f\) is surjective

To show that \(f\) is surjective, we pick an arbitrary element in the codomain and show that it has a preimage under f. Let \(y \in \cup_{i\in I}\{B_i\}\). Since \(f_i\) is surjective, then it means that is has a preimage \(x\) in \(\cup_{i \in I}\{A_i\}\) such that \(f_i(x) = y\). Then it follows that \(f(x) = f_i(x) = y\) [Using the definition of \(f\)]. And so, \(y\) has a preimage under \(f\). Since \(y\) is arbitrary, then we have shown the for all \(y \in \cup_{i\in I}B_i\), \(y\) has a preimage \(x \in \{A_{i \in I}\}\). Thus, \(f\) is surjective.

Claim: \(f\) is bijective

Since \(f\) is surjective and injective, therefore \(f\) is bijective. This means \(f\) is a bijection between \(A_i\) and \(B_i\). Therefore \(\cup_{i\in I}A_i \sim \cup_{i\in I}B\).

\(\text{QED}\).
